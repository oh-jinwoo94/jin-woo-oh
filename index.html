About

From sequence to tissue

During my PhD at Johns Hopkins (2018–2024), I developed mathematical models and computational methods to study regulatory genome evolution, including gkm-align, a computational model for identifying functionally conserved regulatory elements across evolutionarily distant mammals, and contributed to large-scale ENCODE/IGVF CRISPRi perturbation screens to map gene regulatory networks.

At Lunit (2024–present), I apply these computational frameworks to spatial tumor biology, integrating histological morphology and spatial transcriptomics to study the tumor microenvironment and mechanisms of therapeutic resistance in cancer.

My broader goal is to develop quantitative models of how regulatory genomic information drives tissue self-organization and how its misregulation causes disease. The research spans sequence-level regulatory grammar, gene regulatory network dynamics, and the spatial patterning of cell states in development and cancer.

Cell-state transitions in development are governed by cooperative autoregulation among core transcription factors. Using the transition from embryonic stem cells (ESC) to definitive endoderm (DE) as a model system, a CRISPRi screen targeting enhancers of core DE transcription factors revealed that perturbation delays transition rather than preventing it. Cells with perturbed enhancers transition more slowly but ultimately reach the target state. This temporal pattern is consistent with a bistable switch, where the two cell states represent stable fixed points and the transition requires crossing an activation barrier.

A discrete stochastic model was constructed using the Gillespie algorithm, where cooperative TF–enhancer binding drives autocatalytic TF production. Stimulus strength controls whether cells cross the transition threshold; the inhibition parameter models CRISPRi-mediated reduction in enhancer activity. Weak perturbation slows the transition without preventing it; strong perturbation collapses it entirely, reproducing the temporal dynamics observed in the screen.

For more detail, see Nature Genetics 2023.
